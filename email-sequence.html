Email 2 · Day 2

Subject: Year one mistake most holders make

Preheader: And why the structure they pick is the wrong one.

Body:

Most year-one crypto holders ask the wrong first question.

The question they ask: "Which trade should I make."

The question they should ask: "Which entity holds the position."

Same coin. Same buy date. Same exit date. Different entity = different tax treatment, different protection layer, different exit flexibility.

The default entity for most year-one holders is themselves. As in, the holdings sit in a personal wallet under a personal SSN with no entity layer between the position and the IRS. That's the cheapest setup, and for the holder with under $50K total in crypto, it's often the right setup.

The mistake happens at the wealth threshold. Once a position crosses $100K to $250K, the personal-wallet default starts to cost more than the structure under it would. Capital gains, estate exposure, asset-protection gaps, all of it compounds.

The two questions to ask once you cross the threshold:

  1. Custody vs control. Who actually holds the keys? Who can sign on the position?
  2. Entity vs personal. Is the position held by an entity that has a different tax / protection / estate treatment than your personal SSN?

The framework I teach inside the curriculum walks through five entity layers and which one fits which holder type. The PDF covered the surface. The curriculum covers the depth.

Email 3 · Day 4

Subject: The trust layer most holders skip

Preheader: And the structural reason it matters more once you cross $250K.

Body:

There's a tier of holder that doesn't know they're operating without the layer they need.

That tier is the holder with $250K to $2M in crypto, holding personally, with no trust mechanism over the position. The market treats them like they have a sophisticated stack. The IRS treats them like a personal taxable account. The estate planner treats them like the position doesn't exist until they ask.

The trust layer is the bridge between the personal-holding default and the structured-operator setup.

Three things a private trust does that a personal wallet doesn't:

  1. Separates the holder from the holdings. Asset-protection layer between you and the position.
  2. Changes the estate treatment. What happens to the position when the holder passes. Personal-holding default = probate. Trust-held = whatever the trust document says.
  3. Adjusts the tax-event timing. Depending on the trust type, the tax events get spread differently across years.

Subject: Why the sequence matters more than the structures

Preheader: Stack order is the operator's secret.

Body:

Seven tax structures. Each one is publicly available.

The reason most holders still get the tax treatment wrong even after they learn the structures: the sequence.

The structures don't compound additively. They compound in order. The personal entity in year one is the foundation. The LLC layer in year two is the protection. The trust mechanism in year three or four (depending on holder type) is the preservation. The tax-aware exit timing in year five or six is the extension. If you install the trust before you have the LLC, the trust mechanism is operating against an unprotected entity. If you install the LLC before the position is significant enough to justify it, the LLC is operating against a position that doesn't need it.

The sequence is the operator's leverage point. The structures are the tools. The sequence is the order you pick them up.
